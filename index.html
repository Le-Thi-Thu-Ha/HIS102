Post 1: Echoes of Excess at Versailles

Posted: Jul 14, 1789 (The Storm)

Standing here, in the Hall of Mirrors, it’s impossible to reconcile this dazzling opulence with the starvation in Paris. This palace, built to showcase absolute power, became a symbol of the monarchy's detachment. The sheer scale—700 rooms and endless gardens—makes the subsequent fall of Louis XVI almost inevitable. It's a gold-plated bubble that finally burst.

Post 2: The Fall of Citizen Capet

Posted: Jan 21, 1793 (A Nation Divided)

The Place de la Révolution (now Place de la Concorde) felt electric. The execution of Louis XVI, or 'Citizen Capet,' wasn't just a regicide; it was the irreversible death of absolute monarchy in France. His indecisiveness and the disastrous flight to Varennes sealed his fate. The guillotine’s blade dropped, and with it, the foundation of the First Republic was violently laid. The crowd's cheer was deafening.

Post 3: The Dark Turn of the Terror

Posted: Sept 5, 1793 (The Law of Suspects)

The idealism of 'Liberty' has curdled into paranoia under Robespierre's Committee of Public Safety. The 'Law of Suspects' made enemies of everyone. An estimated 17,000 official executions occurred, turning revolutionary fervor into state-sanctioned mayhem. This ten-month purge, characterized by the chilling efficiency of the guillotine, shows the tragic speed at which a fight for rights can devolve into pure, chaotic fear.
